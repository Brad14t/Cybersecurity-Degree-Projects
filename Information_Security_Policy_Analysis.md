# Introduction

Information security policies provide guidelines and procedures for how employees should handle organizational data. These policies and procedures are important as they help keep data private and mitigate potential liability for data breaches. As a cybersecurity professional, you will often create and modify informational security policies to help the organization comply with legal regulations and cybersecurity standards.

In this task, you will act as a cybersecurity professional reviewing an organization's information security policy, with a focus on data privacy and regulatory compliance.

# My Essay

For this assignment, I have selected TeleMedica as the focus organization for my analysis
of data privacy concepts as outlined in their Information Security Policy. TeleMedica is a
telehealth services provider, founded in 2015. The company operates a secure, scalable platform
that connects patients with doctors, specialists, and mental health professionals worldwide.
TeleMedica operations span multiple US locations, as well as London, and collaborate
internationally with its sister company MediCom GmbH, located in Berlin, Germany.
TeleMedica’s approach to data privacy is captured within its Data Protection and Privacy
Policy. Which defines data privacy as the obligation to safeguard confidentiality, integrity, and
availability of sensitive health information in compliance with applicable laws and regulations.
The policy establishes all patient data must be processed lawfully, transparently, and securely.
This reflects a commitment to both regulatory compliance and ethical stewardship of Personal
Health Information (PHI).
Given the nature of TeleMedica’s services, a wide range of sensitive data is handled daily.
These include patient health records generated through remote consultations, AI-powered
diagnostics, and chronic disease management programs. Mental health data, which is collected
via telepsychiatry and mental health support services. Cross-border healthcare data is shared
between TeleMedica and MediCom GmbH as part of international health service delivery.
Operational and system data, which will be stored across cloud-based servers, will be transmitted
through encrypted networks.
Data is at the core of TeleMedica’s business model. The organization's ability to deliver
real-time video consultations, AI-driven diagnostics, and virtual chronic disease management
depends entirely on the secure and reliable handling of patient information. Any exposure of this
data would violate patients' privacy, disrupt continuity of care, damage organizational trust, and
result in significant legal and regulatory consequences. Since TeleMedica spans multiple
international locations, each country has its own privacy laws. Protecting this data is therefore
not simply a compliance requirement, but a foundational operational necessity.
The first framework that appears to have shaped TeleMedica’s policy is the National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework. The NIST CSF is a
widely adopted standard that organizes cybersecurity activities into 5 core functions: Identify,
Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. This promotes a structured risk-based approach to
managing cybersecurity threats across an organization’s people, processes, and technologies.
Evidence that supports TeleMedica using this framework is, within their incident response
policy. It explicitly states that incident response should follow the “Detect, Analyze, Contain,
Eradicate, and Recover (DACER)” methodology (TeleMedica, 2024). This structured approach
closely mirrors the NIST CSF’s detect-and-respond functions. Demonstrating a deliberate,
phased methodology for identifying and resolving security threats. This same policy further
supports this alignment by establishing a Security Operations Center (SOC) with automatic alerts
for unusual activity. Directly reflecting NIST’s emphasis on continuous monitoring and timely
detection.
The second framework evident in TeleMedica’s policy is ISO/IEC 27001, the
internationally recognized standard for Information Security Management Systems. ISO 27001
requires organizations to implement systemic controls across access management, data handling,
risk assessment, operational continuity, periodic audits, and to put an emphasis on the least
privilege principle. Supporting evidence from TeleMedica’s access control policy states that
“access to the patient information systems is restricted based on job function, adhering to the
least privileged principle.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This is exactly mirrored from ISO 27001’s access
control domain (Annex A, Control 9). Further support, the TeleMedica team is mandated to
perform biannual audits of all user access levels. This also reflects ISO 27001’s requirements for
regular review and monitoring of access rights.
The first security standard that clearly informed TeleMedica’s policy is HIPAA,
specifically the Security Rule and Privacy Rule. HIPAA establishes the national standard in the
US for protecting sensitive patient health information. Requiring organizations to implement
administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and
availability of all electronic protected health information. HIPAA compliance is mandatory for
TeleMedica, as it operates across multiple states. Supporting evidence from TeleMedica’s data
protection and privacy policy opens by stating its purpose is to “safeguard the confidentiality,
integrity, and availability of sensitive health information in compliance with applicable laws and
privacy regulations.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This security rule is what HIPAA is built on.
Furthermore, the policy mandates that restricted data, including patient records, be encrypted and
stored in a secure data vault, and any data transfer be through a VPN. Both of which correspond
to HIPAA’s technical safeguard requirements.
The second security standard reflected in TeleMedica’s policy is the use of encryption
protocols for data transit. Specifically referencing Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) as the standard
for governing how data is moved between TeleMedica’s systems. Supporting evidence within
TeleMedica’s data protection and privacy policy states under its infrastructure overview, “data in
transit is encrypted with secure sockets layer version 2.0 (SSL v2) protocols.” (TeleMedica,
2024) This directly demonstrates that a specific named encryption standard was incorporated into
the policy’s infrastructure design. These two standards, HIPAA and SSL, reflect TeleMedica’s
dual obligation to meet both regulatory requirements specific to the healthcare industry and
technical security baselines. Together they show the organization's stated commitment to lawful,
transparent, and secure data processing.
First best practice from within the information security policy is data classification and
labeling. TeleMedica’s data protection and privacy policy procedures state that “employees use
an enterprise-approved labeling tool to label documents as public, internal, confidential, or
restricted.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This is effective because data classification is a foundational best
practice that ensures security controls are applied proportionately to the sensitivity of the data
being protected. Without this classification, organizations risk either over-exposing highly
sensitive data or misallocating security resources. With these tiered labels, TeleMedica
establishes a clear framework for how data should be handled, stored, and transmitted. Reducing
the likelihood of accidental exposure or unauthorized access to very secure patient data. This
practice aligns with the industry standard ISO/ IEC 27001, specifically the classification of data
(Annex A Control 5.12). Which requires organizations to classify information according to legal
requirements.
The next best practice is quarterly access privilege audits. Their data protection and
privacy policy states that “the IT security team conducts quarterly audits of access privileges.”
(TeleMedica, 2024) The organization has committed to doing biannual audits. This is effective
because regular auditing of access privileges is a critical safeguard against insider threats and
privilege creep, which could lead to a user gaining access beyond their role. Periodic audits
allow the organization to identify and revoke unnecessary or excessive access before an exploit
can be exploited, ensuring the least privilege principle remains enforced. This practice is directly
supported by the industry standard of NIST Special Publication 800-53. In Control AC-2
(Account Management). This requires organizations to review user accounts at a defined
frequency. This NIST standard is widely recognized as the benchmark for federal and enterpriselevel access management controls.
The final best practice is secure data destruction. TeleMedica’s data protection and
privacy procedure states that “sensitive data is permanently deleted from the system using an
enterprise-approved data destruction tool.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This is effective because secure
data destruction is an essential but often overlooked component of a comprehensive data
protection strategy. Simply deleting a file or decommissioning a device does not render the data
irrecoverable. Without deliberate, verified, destructive methods, sensitive data can persist. By
mandating the use of enterprise-level tools, TeleMedica ensures that data is rendered
permanently unreadable. This practice aligns with the industry standard of NIST Special
Publication 800-88 (Guideline for Media Sanitization). Which provides organizations with a
framework for selecting appropriate sanitization techniques. This standard establishes that
proper sanitization is a necessary control to prevent unauthorized recovery of sensitive
information.
Together, the three practices of data classification, access privilege auditing, and secure
data destruction. Collectively address the full lifecycle of sensitive data. Together, they reflect a
layered, defense-in-depth approach.
The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), along with the 2020 addition of the
California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), is a state-level privacy law that grants California
residents significant rights over their personal data. Organizations that collect personal data from
California residents, regardless of their headquarters, are required to comply with the CCPA if
they meet certain thresholds for data volume or revenue. Since TeleMedica’s primary data
storage location is explicitly identified in the policy as California, personal information collected
from residents is subject to this law. An example of policy compliance is the data protection and
privacy policy, which states, “Only essential data is collected, and employees must review data
fields quarterly to ensure relevance.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This practice of collecting only what's
necessary directly reflects the CCPA’s principle of limiting data collection and retention to what
is necessary. Furthermore, the enterprise-approved tool for data destruction supports compliance
with the CCPA’s right to deletion requirement.
At the national level, TeleMedica is subject to Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission
Act. This prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce. The FTC applies Section 5
to data security and takes enforcement action against organizations that fail to implement
reasonable security measures to protect consumer data. In TeleMedica’s acceptable use policy, it
states that “TeleMedica’s network – including the communication platform and company-issued
devices is to be solely for business purposes,” (TeleMedica, 2024) and that “security will audit
for unauthorized software installations quarterly.” (TeleMedica, 2024) These controls
demonstrate that TeleMedica has implemented reasonable organizational safeguards to prevent
unauthorized access.
Internationally, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is the most
comprehensive data privacy law. It applies to any organization, regardless of where it is based,
that processes personal data of individuals located in the European Union Economic Area. GDPR
establishes strict requirements around lawful data protection. TeleMedica is subject to GDPR
because it actively collaborates with MediCom GmbH, its sister company in Berlin, Germany. In
the data protection and privacy policy, it states, “data transmission between TeleMedica systems,
the MediCom GmbH platform, and third-party health providers will occur over a VPN.”
(TeleMedica, 2024) The transmission of data over a VPN directly supports GDPR compliance
under article 32.
An industry-specific law would be HIPAA. Which is the primary industry-specific federal
law governing the protection of protected health information? TeleMedica’s access control policy
states that "access to the patient information systems is restricted based on job function, adhering
to the least privilege principle.” (TeleMedica, 2024) This role-based access control satisfies
HIPAA’s security rule technical safeguard requirement.
The first instance of TeleMedica’s failure to maintain or meet known security standards
or legal regulations is the use of the deprecated SSL v2.0 for data-in-transit encryption. While the
organization has stated that it uses encryption for data in transit, which is good. The specific
protocol identified is critically outdated and has been formally deprecated. This fails to meet
established standards because SSL v2.0 was officially prohibited by the Internet Engineering
Task Force in 2011 through RFC 6176. This is because of well-known vulnerabilities. The
current industry accepted standard for securing data in transit are TLS 1.2 and TLS 1.3, as
recommended by NIST 800-52. Instance two is that the password policy lacks sufficient
authentication requirements. The organization's policy requires users to change their passwords
every 90 days. While this is a good start, it doesn’t require complexity, length, or MFA. It fails to
meet NIST 800-63B, the current authoritative federal standard for authentication. The changes
needed would require greater complexity, longer length, and some form of MFA. The third
instance is the inadequate backup frequency under the disaster recovery policy. Currently,
weekly backups of critical data are stored in London’s failover center, with quarterly recovery
testing. While the existing backup and disaster recovery plan is good control. The frequency is a
gap for the operational criticality of data from TeleMedica. It fails because if any attack or
system goes down, you will lose up to 7 days of data. For healthcare systems, the recovery point
objective should be in hours, not days.
Proactive measures for deprecated SSL v2.0, to be switched to TLS 1.3. TeleMedica
should immediately update the policy to replace SSL v2.0 with TLS 1.3, as the mandated
protocol for all data in transit encryption. This policy should apply to all systems, including the
MediCom GmbH platform and third-party health providers. This will correct the policy and
enhance security posture by eliminating the cryptographic weakness in SSL v2.0. This included
weak cipher suites, susceptibility to man-in-the-middle attacks, and protocol downgrading. One
of the biggest improvements to the security posture would be with the inclusion of TLS 1.3 even
if the session key is later compromised, past communications remain protected. It also
significantly reduces the attack surface for known SSL exploits.
Recommendation two would be to implement multi-factor authentication and strengthen
the password policy. TeleMedica should implement mandatory MFA for all employees’ access to
systems. In addition, the password policy needs to be updated to improve complexity, length, and
the use of special characters. This corrects the policy and enhances security posture by not
relying on a single authentication method and a password that is more difficult to crack. With
MFA, even if a user has their credentials compromised, the attacker would still need a way of
performing the secondary authentication.
Recommendation three is to increase backup frequency to daily and implement monthly
recovery testing. TeleMedica should revise its disaster recovery policy to increase critical data
backups from weekly to daily. With backups stored in the London failover center. Additionally,
recovery testing should be increased from quarterly to monthly for critical systems. The revision
of the policy should also establish a formally documented recovery objective, and recovery time
objective. This corrects the policy and enhances security posture by reducing the window of
potentially lost data from a week to a day. Continual recovery testing on a monthly schedule
provides a well-known environment where you know recovery works and how it does. This
strengthens the security posture because, in the event of a ransomware attack, system failure, or
natural disaster, the organization can restore patient data and resume care with minimal
disruption.
