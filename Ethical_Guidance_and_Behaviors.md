# My Essay

A. A professional code of ethics is a formal set of principles and standards that govern the
conduct of individuals. It’s more of a moral compass, guiding people to navigate complex
decisions by providing clear expectations for behavior, accountability, and values.
Providing a professional code of ethics helps guide behavior in several ways. First, it
establishes a clear standard by defining acceptable and unacceptable conduct, removing
uncertainty in similar and difficult situations. For example, a software engineer
consulting a company with a clear code of ethics must prioritize public well-being and
user privacy above business decisions, such as refusing to implement systems that
secretly monitor and collect data without proper consent. These standards create
consistency across the board, regardless of individual employers. Second, providing a
decision-making framework can allow the user to evaluate their choices against a
published shared moral standard. Instead of relying on personal judgment, which can be
subjective and influence changes that are not appropriate to the shared moral standard.
Third, creating accountability ensures everyone knows what isn’t allowed and what the
appropriate consequences are. Codes establish a baseline for evaluating conduct. Lastly,
over time, with the addition of these codes, professional culture will transition into
always performing the ethical norm. Training will be mandated so that these behaviors
become second nature rather than just following a rule.
The two key benefits of a profession are public trust and professional unity. The addition
of codes of ethics shows the public that the company holds itself to a high standard. This
builds trust because they know enforceable codes bind practitioners. Unity comes along
with a shared code that everyone is trained on and practices together. If everyone is
abiding by the same code, it limits the factors that could pressure users into unethical
behavior. It establishes a common language of values.
B. My ethical principles for cybersecurity are general ethics principles and compliance with
code. General ethics principles are a foundation for all cybersecurity analysts, because the
role carries significant responsibilities. An analyst has access to and responsibility for
computer systems, data, and the people who use them. “An essential aim of computing
professionals is to minimize negative consequences of computing, including threats to
health, safety, personal security, and privacy.” (Association for Computing Machinery,
2018) This statement states how an analyst should approach their work. A cybersecurity
analyst handles sensitive personal data, monitors network traffic, and reveals
vulnerabilities. General ethical principles guide how access should be handled. Principle
1.6 emphasizes that these professionals “should only use personal information for
legitimate ends and without violating the rights of individuals and groups.” (Association
for Computing Machinery, 2018) In short, the analyst encounters personal information.
They have a responsibility only to use that data to fulfill the security objective.
Compliance with code is equally important for an analyst, as the cybersecurity field
operates within strict legal and professional standards. Principle 4.1 states, “The future of
computing depends on both technical and ethical excellence.” Being a skilled analyst
isn’t enough; skills must also align with and support ethical accountability. In
cybersecurity, compliance means ensuring all actions are conducted within authorized
and legal boundaries. Alongside that, they are also to recognize when others are acting
unethically. Compliance with the code ultimately protects the analyst and the public.

1. One of the most serious negative impacts of AI bias is when biased algorithms are
used to make or influence high-stakes decisions. Like hiring, lending capital, criminal
sentencing, and healthcare. When an algorithm is trained on historical data that
already reflects societal inequalities, it will learn and amplify those inequalities. An
example of this was the COMPAS algorithm. Used in the U.S. criminal justice system
to predict the likelihood of reoffending. Later studies found the tool was significantly
more likely to falsely flag black defendants as a higher risk compared to other
defendants. Misinformation like this is invisible to most people, and they won't fully
understand why that decision was made. “The score proved remarkably unreliable in
forecasting violent crime: Only 20 percent of the people predicted to commit violent
crimes actually went on to do so.” (Andwin et al., 2016) The consequences of this
bias resulted in harsher sentences, denied parole, and other discrepancies. Unlike
human bias, AI bias can affect thousands or even millions of people at once.
Another significant impact of AI is the spread of AI-generated misinformation,
enabling the production of false narratives at an unknown scale and speed. Unlike
traditional misinformation designed to manipulate the public, AI misinformation is
cheaper, highly convincing, and difficult to detect. Making it a serious threat to public
trust.
2. With the power of AI tools, like the ones that can generate realistic images, audio, and
or video. These tools can produce what's called deepfakes. This creates a significant
risk of misinformation given the internet's scale. There is no saying how fast this
misinformation can spread. With the speed of spread, these tools are incredibly
convincing, cheap, and difficult to detect. First, false narratives can directly harm
individuals. If someone were to fabricate images or statements about a real person
and spread them as true, it could directly damage their reputation, incite harassment,
and more. Second, the widespread misinformation slowly erodes public trust. Making
it hard for most to distinguish the difference between fact and fiction.
The ACM code of ethics can address discriminatory AI decisions with several of its
core principles. Principle 1.4 requires fairness and action to prevent discrimination.
This code states that "the use of information and technology may cause new, or
enhance existing, inequities" (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018) and calls
for professionals to “take action to avoid creating systems or technologies that
disenfranchise or oppress people.” (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018)
This places ethical obligations on developers, deployers, and AI companies to
actively audit their systems for bias and take the appropriate corrective actions.
Principle 1.2, avoiding harm, is further reinforced by requiring professionals to
consider the potential impacts of their decisions carefully. When an AI system is
outputting bias or misinformation, the bias cannot simply be ignored. Principle 1.2,
which contributes to society and human well-being, helps ensure their work respects
diversity. If a system disadvantages minority groups, it fails this standard entirely.
Together, these principles push professionals to move beyond technical performance
metrics and take responsibility for the real-world social outcomes of the systems they
built. Using ACM code as a meaningful tool in the fight against AI bias.
