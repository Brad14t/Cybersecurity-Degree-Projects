A.
1. The selected scenario involves a manager at a mid-sized company who accidentally
sends a confidential email containing all employees’salaries, bonuses, and
performance evaluations to the entire department. Rather than to the intended single
recipient. The manager, despite attempting to retract the email, several employees had
already viewed the confidential information before the email could have been
recalled.
The central ethical issue in this scenario is the breach of employee privacy and
confidentiality. All employees of an organization have a reasonable expectation that
their personal financial and performance information will be kept private. Only
allowing access to those with a legitimate work need. For example, upper
management and HR. The managers’ accidental actions violated that expectation by
exposing the employee’s sensitive data to colleagues who lacked the authority to view
it. Even if the actions are unintentional, they are still considered an ethical failure. As
a manager, you have a duty of care to protect that information.
2. The breach of employee privacy and confidentiality is problematic on several levels.
First is confidential employee data, such as salaries, bonuses, and performance
evaluations. These were never intended to be shared broadly, and their exposure
undermines employees' trust. Second, the disclosure revealed pay disparities across
job levels. This further lowers employees’ trust in the employer. This can also create
feelings of resentment, unfairness, and demotivation among the employees. Third, the
company may be subject to legal exposure, as many jurisdictions have workplace
privacy laws and data protection regulations.
Several parties are affected by the breach. The employees whose data was exposed
are the most directly harmed. Their private details were shared without consent.
Employees who viewed the email are also affected; they now possess information that
could influence workplace relations and morale. The manager who’s responsible faces
professional and possibly legal consequences. Not only does it damage their
credibility and trust, but it also damages their relationship with upper management.
Finally, the company as a whole is impacted. Through potential reputational harm,
legal liability, and impacted workplace culture.
3. The primary concern at play in this scenario is data privacy, with a secondary concern
of data access. Data privacy refers to individuals' right to control how their personal
information is collected, stored, and shared. In this scenario, data privacy was directly
violated; each employee expects their personal information to be accessible only to
authorized viewers. This isn’t just a personal expectation; this is a foundational
principle of responsible data handling within an organization.
Data access also played a significant role in this breach. Proper data access protocols
could have easily ensured that sensitive information could be accessed only by
previously authorized individuals. The point of weakness was the manager's ability to
send this information to everyone. This isn’t just human error; it also points to a
significant vulnerability within the organization—protocols for authorizing the
transmission of specific personal data, or protocols for stricter access to specific data.
A simple mistake in this situation has resulted in a significant breach.
Both privacy and access have the same question using the PAPA model: “What
safeguards are in place to protect a person's privacy?” and “What safeguards exist for
information access?” (Den Adams, 2009) This points to a huge weakness within the
company, since both questions can be answered even without safeguards in place.
B.
1. The ethical framework applied is deontology. Deontology is a duty-based ethical
framework that holds that the morality of an action is determined by whether it
adheres to a set of rules or duties, regardless of the outcome. Essentially, doing the
right thing is an obligation, not a calculation of consequences. From a deontological
standpoint, the manager in this scenario had an unconditional duty to protect the
confidentiality of employee records. That duty isn’t because of what might happen if
violated, but because safeguarding private information is an inherent moral obligation
when managing others. I believe the company can use the approach of Formula of
Humanity “Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or
in the person of another, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same
time as an end.” (Panopto, 2026)
A deontological response would focus on fulfilling the obligations that follow after
the breach, not on the consequences. First, the manager has a duty to be fully
transparent. Promptly notifying all affected parties, with full transparency on what
was shared, to whom, and what happened. Next, the manager has a duty to improve
safeguards, implementing stronger data-handling procedures.
2. A secondary ethical framework applied here is utilitarianism. Utilitarianism focuses
on the right course of action for the greater good, meaning the greater number of
people. “What will lead to the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people?”
(Panopto, 2026) These two frameworks would arrive at the same practical response to
this scenario. An utilitarian would also support full transparency with the affected
users, prompt notification to HR, and implement stronger data protection. The
reasoning behind these actions would differ significantly, where a deontologist
pursues transparency because honesty is a moral duty. An utilitarian pursues it
because open communication is likely to minimize harm, restore trust, and produce a
better overall outcome for the company and its employees. However, from an external
point of view, these actions seem the same. The justifications driving them are
drastically different.
